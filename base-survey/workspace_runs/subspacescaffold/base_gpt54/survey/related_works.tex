\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

\paragraph{Federated learning under heterogeneity.}
Federated learning (FL) was popularized by FedAvg, which alternates multiple local SGD steps with periodic model averaging across clients~\cite{mcmahan2017communication}. While simple and communication-efficient, FedAvg is well known to degrade under statistically heterogeneous (non-IID) data because local training trajectories drift toward client-specific optima~\cite{hsu2019measuring,karimireddy2020scaffold,sahu2020fedprox,karimireddy2020mime,wang2020fednova,acar2021feddyn,li2021moon}. A broad line of work therefore aims to control or regularize this drift. FedProx adds a proximal term to stabilize local optimization under systems and data heterogeneity~\cite{sahu2020fedprox}. SCAFFOLD introduces client and server control variates to reduce the bias induced by local updates and provides strong guarantees under heterogeneity~\cite{karimireddy2020scaffold}. Subsequent methods such as FedNova, MIME, MOON, and FedDyn further address objective inconsistency, mimic centralized optimization dynamics, or impose representation/model regularization~\cite{wang2020fednova,karimireddy2020mime,li2021moon,acar2021feddyn}. However, these methods typically operate in the full parameter space and often maintain auxiliary states of dimension $d$, such as control variates, server statistics, or regularization-related memories. For modern large models, this full-dimensional state becomes a major practical bottleneck in both client memory and communication.

\paragraph{Communication compression in federated and distributed optimization.}
To reduce the cost of transmitting high-dimensional updates, another major thread studies communication compression through quantization, sparsification, and low-precision signaling~\cite{alistarh2017qsgd,bernstein2018signsgd,bernstein2019majority,lin2018deep,stich2018memory,mishchenko2019diana,khirirat2019memory,stich2020heterogeneous,sahu2021rethinking,condat2022efbv}. In federated settings, representative approaches include periodic averaging with quantization (FedPAQ)~\cite{reisizadeh2020fedpaq}, sparse ternary compression and related robust compressed FL schemes~\cite{sattler2020robust,xu2021ternary,mora2022structured}, and more recent adaptive quantization/sparsification methods for heterogeneous wireless or edge settings~\cite{makarenko2022adaptive,zakerinia2022communication,marnissi2024adaptive,oh2024overtheair,bao2025efskip}. A key practical ingredient in many biased compression schemes is error feedback (EF), which accumulates the residual compression error to recover convergence and accuracy~\cite{stich2018memory,khirirat2019memory,condat2022efbv}. Yet EF itself requires storing an additional error vector in $\mathbb{R}^d$, offsetting part of the communication gains with substantial client-side memory overhead. Moreover, under heterogeneous local objectives, compression noise can compound client drift, making the interaction between heterogeneity mitigation and compression substantially more delicate~\cite{stich2020heterogeneous,zakerinia2022communication,bao2025efskip}. Thus, although compression reduces transmitted bits, many existing methods still rely on full-dimensional historical states and do not fundamentally remove the memory bottleneck.

\paragraph{Subspace and low-rank learning for efficient training.}
A third direction seeks to reduce optimization cost by restricting updates to a low-dimensional subspace or low-rank parameterization. In centralized training, gradient low-rank projection methods such as GaLore show that optimizer states can be maintained in compact projected spaces while preserving strong empirical performance~\cite{zhao2024galore,su2025galore2,zhang2024qgalore,hao2024flora,pan2025unbiased}. Closely related parameter-efficient adaptation methods, especially LoRA-style low-rank updates, also motivate the use of structured low-dimensional update spaces for large models~\cite{hu2022lora,xia2024chainlora,wang2023multilora,renduchintala2023tiedlora}. These ideas have recently entered federated settings through subspace or low-rank adaptation methods such as FedSub, selective LoRA aggregation, and ILoRA, as well as gradient-subspace based federated fine-tuning for large models~\cite{zhang2025fedsub,guo2024selective,zhou2025ilora,mahla2024exploring}. Their common promise is appealing: by training and communicating only low-dimensional factors or projected updates, they can reduce communication, computation, and memory simultaneously. Nevertheless, existing subspace FL methods are largely orthogonal to SCAFFOLD-style variance reduction. In particular, when the active subspace changes across rounds or clients, historical control variates accumulated in previous coordinates need not remain valid in the new subspace. This mismatch makes a naive combination of subspace optimization with control-variate correction ill-posed. Our work is motivated exactly by this gap: to obtain the heterogeneity robustness of variance reduction together with the memory and communication benefits of subspace learning, without relying on full-dimensional auxiliary states.
